Unlock the proven ways to reach depth

Why do we need depth in reading

Depth transforms what we know, our attitudes toward learning, and our potential to make sense of new ideas.

When students attain deep understanding of a text, they can:

  1. Explain the text thoroughly (establish meaning)
  2. Analyze the text (assess, compare, ask why and how)
  3. Apply ideas from the text in new ways (such as by writing an original essay, creating a project based on ideas from the text, etc.)

When students reach depth, they know it, and they feel the joy of it. The learning experience when reading for depth is not only more meaningful and memorable, but it also pays dividends into the future as an anchor for further learning.

Challenges in deep reading

Reading for depth means thinking harder and focusing longer than what is natural. Our brains are wired for efficiency and conservation of energy. The vast majority of thinking happens like a reflex: it's fast and automatic. This is why switching gears to thinking in a slow, deliberate way requires significant time and effort.

There are three key roadblocks that prevent students from achieving depth in their reading:

  1. Students tend to read at surface level. Reading for depth requires focused attention, the willingness to slow down and re-read certain passages, and a critical perspective that questions, evaluates, and engages in a discourse with the text. This way of thinking is not obvious and often will not happen without guidance and structure.
  2. Students cannot access the text at a basic level due to gaps in content knowledge, language barriers, or reading impairments. Even with the best of intentions, students who cannot make sense of what they read will not achieve depth.
  3. Students have no idea whether they are on track with how they analyze and make sense of the text. Although students may put in a high level of effort, they continue to repeat the same mistakes, relying on misconceptions.

What’s ASR and how it helps

Our solution to overcome the roadblocks in deep reading is ASR (Activate, Support, Reveal). Actively Learn helps teachers activate, support, and reveal student thinking.

Activate Thinking

Teachers activate students’ thinking by providing a purpose, modeling close reading, asking higher-order questions, facilitating peer discussion, and encouraging students to annotate while they read. With Actively Learn, teachers help students engage and collaborate more, which helps them persevere through a complex text.

Support Thinking

Many factors can derail students from achieving depth in their reading. Actively Learn amplifies instructional guidance to help overcome insufficient background knowledge, vocabulary gaps, disfluency, and more. Technology helps students make sense of the words by offering dyslexic settings, built-in foreign language dictionaries, and text-to-speech.

Reveal Thinking

Targeted and effective feedback depends on teachers' ability to see how students are thinking and struggling as they construct knowledge. Revealing thinking consists of having students write extensively (both annotations and question responses) while they read, encouraging metacognition, and providing effective feedback for deeper learning.
